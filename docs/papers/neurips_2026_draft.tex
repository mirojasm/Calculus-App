\begin{abstract}
Evaluating whether LLM agents genuinely collaborate---rather than merely
exchange outputs---requires metrics beyond task accuracy. We introduce the
Collaborative Phase Profile (\CPP), a PISA-grounded framework measuring
collaborative depth, quality, Phase-A exploration, and collaborative yield,
and the \CIDI{} benchmark: $140$ MATH problems with information splits
designed to induce epistemic interdependence. Across $1{,}967$ conversations on \CIDI-amenable problems,
active-learner framing is associated with a large \CDI{} gain ($0.362 \to 0.613$,
$d=1.30$, $p<0.001$) and more than doubles fully coupled collaboration ($11\%$ to $24\%$),
while peer-awareness alone yields no practically meaningful gain after correction.
Two full-corpus ablations show that neither active-learner framing without the
split (C\textsubscript{FULL}) nor expert framing with the split (C\textsubscript{EXP})
reproduces this pattern, consistent with a synergy between structural interdependence
and communicative role framing. Agent Epistemic Contribution (\AEC) separates
structural interdependence from activated epistemic necessity: $85\%$ of problems
are both-solo-zero, yet only $25\%$ become activated under the strongest tested
framing (upper-bound estimate; see \S4.1). We release \CPP, \CIDI{} splits, conversation logs, scoring scripts,
\AEC{} scripts, and a benchmark card documenting corpus conditionality and intended use.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Multi-agent systems built on LLMs have demonstrated remarkable
capabilities in code generation \citep{hong2023metagpt},
debate-based reasoning \citep{liang2023encouraging, du2023improving},
and socially grounded interaction \citep{park2023generative}. In most of these systems,
collaboration is designed at the \emph{task level}: agents receive
different tools, different roles in a pipeline, or access to different
data shards. Relatively little attention has been paid to whether the
\emph{epistemic stance} an agent adopts---how it positions itself
cognitively with respect to its own knowledge and its partner's
knowledge---can alter the quality of emergent collaboration.

This question has both evaluation and design implications. On the
evaluation side, benchmarking collaborative agents requires metrics
that can distinguish genuine joint reasoning from efficient
answer-delegation---a distinction that task-accuracy scores obscure.
On the design side, research in the learning sciences has long
established that productive collaboration requires participants to
engage in genuine co-construction of understanding rather than
answer-sharing or expertise-transfer
\citep{roschelle1995construction, dillenbourg1999collaborative}.
If LLM agents default to the role of a knowledgeable assistant---which
is precisely what instruction fine-tuning optimises for---eliciting
collaborative behaviour requires an explicit epistemic intervention,
not merely a task decomposition. Developing and validating such
interventions demands evaluation infrastructure that does not yet
exist for LLM multi-agent systems.

We operationalise this intervention through three increasingly
specific framing conditions (see Section~\ref{sec:design}). The
weakest framing (C2) provides no special instructions beyond
handing each agent a partial information packet. The intermediate
framing (C6) additionally makes agents \emph{aware} that a
peer with complementary information exists. The strongest framing (C7) instructs agents to adopt the
communicative stance of an active student: express uncertainty
explicitly, ask clarifying questions, verify understanding aloud before
advancing to solution steps, and treat the partner's contributions as
material to reason about.

To quantify how deeply agents collaborate, we introduce the
Collaborative Phase Profile (\CPP) framework
(Section~\ref{sec:framework}), grounded in the PISA 2015 CPS
competency model \citep{pisa2015cps}. \CPP{} decomposes each
conversation into a $4 \times 3$ matrix of collaborative phases and
competencies and derives four summary statistics: \CDI{} (breadth of
phase engagement), \CQI{} (quality-weighted depth), \PhAQ{} (quality
of joint exploration), and \CY{} (composite yield).

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{\CPP}: a PISA-grounded 12-cell rubric producing \CDI, \CQI, \PhAQ, \CY, and a quadrant taxonomy (COUPLING / PROD\_FAIL / TRIVIAL / COLLAPSE).
    \item \textbf{\CIDI{} benchmark}: $140$ MATH problems with epistemic-interdependence splits verified by \AEC{} ($85\%$ both-solo-zero).
    \item \textbf{Framing study} ($1{,}967$ + $832$ ablation conversations): active-learner framing is associated with a large \CDI{} gain ($d=1.30$); peer-awareness alone is not; neither component alone suffices.
    \item \textbf{\AEC{} decomposition}: separates structural ($85\%$ both-solo-zero) from activated ($25\%$ epistemic necessity) interdependence.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

The PISA 2015 CPS framework characterises collaborative problem solving
along a $4 \times 3$ grid of process phases (Exploring, Representing,
Planning, Monitoring) and collaboration competencies (Shared Understanding,
Action, Team Organisation) \citep{pisa2015cps,graesser2017complex,graesser2018advancing},
with OECD-based tools validated in human-to-agent settings
\citep{rojas2021assessing}. We adapt this grid to LLM-to-LLM dialogues
as the basis for \CPP{} (Section~\ref{sec:framework}).

Prior multi-agent LLM work has explored role-playing cooperation
\citep{li2023camel, hong2023metagpt, qian2023chatdev}, multi-agent
conversation \citep{wu2024autogen}, debate-based reasoning
\citep{liang2023encouraging, du2023improving}, and social-intelligence
evaluation \citep{park2023generative, zhou2023sotopia}. Debate systems
optimise adversarial refinement; social-agent benchmarks assess behaviour;
neither operationalises CPS phases or epistemic interdependence---the
distinction \CPP{} captures as \emph{coordination} vs.\ \emph{collaboration}.

Epistemic interdependence---where no participant can reach the goal
alone---predicts productive collaboration in human learning
\citep{barron2003conditions,johnson2009cooperative}, but capable LLMs may
bypass it by inferring missing information. Communicative role framing
(learner vs.\ expert stance) activates different discourse strategies in
peer tutoring \citep{chi2001learning,king1992facilitating}; whether it
does so in LLM-to-LLM settings is untested. \CIDI{} and C7 operationalise
and test both mechanisms jointly.

\section{The CPP Framework}
\label{sec:framework}

\subsection{The 12-Cell Collaborative Phase Matrix}

We adapt the PISA CPS $4 \times 3$ grid to LLM-to-LLM mathematical
dialogues. Each cell $(P, C)$ for process $P \in \{A, B, C, D\}$ and
competency $C \in \{1, 2, 3\}$ is annotated by a trained scorer on a
0--3 scale using a detailed rubric (see Appendix~\ref{app:rubric}):
\textbf{0} = absent, \textbf{1} = attempted but superficial,
\textbf{2} = substantive but incomplete, \textbf{3} = full and
well-articulated. A cell is considered \emph{active} if its score
$\geq 1$.


\subsection{Summary Metrics}

\paragraph{Collaborative Depth Index (\CDI).}
\begin{equation}
  \CDI = \frac{|\{(P,C) : \text{score}(P,C) \geq 1\}|}{12} \in [0, 1].
\end{equation}
\CDI{} measures \emph{breadth}: what fraction of the 12 cells were
genuinely engaged. A value of $1.0$ means every phase-competency
combination was visited; $0.0$ means the dialogue was entirely
unilateral.

\paragraph{Collaborative Quality Index (\CQI).}
\begin{equation}
  \CQI = \frac{\sum_{P,C} \text{score}(P,C)}{36} \in [0, 1].
\end{equation}
\CQI{} weights by quality: 36 is the maximum possible total score
($12 \times 3$). Unlike \CDI{}, \CQI{} penalises superficial
engagement.

\paragraph{Phase A Quality (\PhAQ).}
\begin{equation}
  \PhAQ = \frac{\sum_{C \in \{1,2,3\}} \text{score}(A, C)}{9} \in [0, 1].
\end{equation}
Process~A is the locus of joint problem-model construction; $\PhAQ = 0$ indicates agents proceeded to solution without any joint exploration.

\paragraph{ATC\textsubscript{CQI} (\ATC).}
We also score against the five ATC21S collaborative competency dimensions \citep{griffin2012assessment} (0--3 each); aggregate $\ATC = \frac{\sum 5\text{ dims}}{15}$.

\paragraph{Collaborative Yield (\CY).}
\begin{equation}
  \CY = 0.35 \cdot \CDI + 0.45 \cdot \mathbb{1}[\text{correct}]
        + 0.20 \cdot \delta_{\text{couple}},
\end{equation}
where $\delta_{\text{couple}} = 1$ iff the conversation is in the COUPLING quadrant.

\subsection{Quadrant Taxonomy}

We embed conversations in a 2D outcome space defined by \CDI{}
(process) and binary correctness (outcome):

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
  \toprule
  \textbf{Quadrant} & \textbf{CDI} & \textbf{Correct} \\
  \midrule
  COUPLING  & $\geq 0.5$ & Yes \\
  PROD\_FAIL & $\geq 0.5$ & No  \\
  TRIVIAL    & $< 0.5$   & Yes \\
  COLLAPSE   & $< 0.5$   & No  \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

COUPLING (high \CDI, correct) is the ideal---deep collaboration \emph{and} success.
PROD\_FAIL (high \CDI, incorrect) is productive failure \citep{kapur2016examining}.
TRIVIAL (low \CDI, correct) implies one agent solved independently.
COLLAPSE (low \CDI, incorrect) is complete failure of both process and outcome.

\subsection{The CIDI Information Split}
\label{sec:cidi}

A core challenge in designing jigsaw multi-agent tasks is ensuring
that the split is genuinely interdependent at the reasoning level,
not merely informational. We introduce Collaborative
Interdependence-Directed Information (\CIDI): an automated pipeline
that uses GPT-4.1 to decompose each problem into three components:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \emph{Shared context}: the problem statement elements visible
  to both agents, including notation and domain.
  \item \emph{Packet A}: information given exclusively to Agent~A,
  chosen so that Agent~A cannot solve the problem without the
  \emph{reasoning output} (not just the raw facts) of Agent~B.
  \item \emph{Packet B}: the symmetric complement for Agent~B.
\end{enumerate}

The key criterion is \emph{reasoning-output dependency}: neither agent
can progress past initial exploration without the other's deductions.
Unlike negative-instruction (``constitutional'') splits---which LLMs
frequently override using general domain knowledge---\CIDI{} uses
positive, non-inferable complementary constraints.

\section{Experimental Design}
\label{sec:design}

\subsection{Problem Corpus Selection (Phase 1)}

We drew problems from the MATH benchmark \citep{hendrycks2021math},
spanning six subject areas (Algebra, Geometry, Number Theory,
Prealgebra, Precalculus, and Counting \& Probability) and five
difficulty levels (MATH levels 1--5), yielding a $6 \times 5 = 30$
cell grid. From an initial pool of 300 problems we applied a
\emph{genuine epistemic filter}: we ran all 300 problems under
condition C7 and retained only those for which $\CDI \geq 0.5$ in at
least two of three replications. The purpose of this filter is to
identify problems for which the \CIDI{} split produces genuine
reasoning-level interdependence---problems that are too easy or too
structurally simple tend to be resolved in one agent's opening message
regardless of framing, making them uninformative for studying
collaborative dynamics. The filter retained $140$ problems
($47\%$ retention rate), distributed across all 30 subject-difficulty
cells with a minimum of 1 problem per cell and a mean of 4.7 problems.

\emph{Selection conditionality.}\enspace Because the filter uses C7
performance to define the corpus, \emph{all estimates are conditional on
\CIDI-amenable problems}: absolute CDI values reflect performance on
problems pre-selected for collaborative potential, and within-corpus
effect sizes may overestimate the benefit expected on an unfiltered sample.
Paired comparisons remain internally valid within this regime.
As a post-hoc inflation check we ran C2 on all $67$ Corpus-1 rejected problems
(\CDI(C7)${}<0.5$): \CDI(C7)${}=0.220$ and \CDI(C2)${}=0.200$
($\Delta=+0.020$ vs.\ $+0.251$ on retained; $12.7\times$ attenuation),
with both conditions declining symmetrically rather than C7 remaining elevated---inconsistent with circular filter inflation. External validity on an independently drawn unfiltered sample remains future work.

\subsection{Conditions (Phase 2)}

All conditions use the same model (GPT-4.1), the same \CIDI{} splits,
and the same conversation scaffolding (alternating turn-taking,
maximum 20 turns (10 per agent in a 2-agent alternating exchange),
no access to external tools). The \emph{only}
manipulation is the system-prompt framing given to each agent.

\paragraph{C2: Natural (L1 baseline).}
Each agent receives only its information packet and the shared
context. No framing about partners, learning, or epistemic role.
Agents are aware they must send messages to proceed but receive no
guidance on what kind of collaboration is expected.

\paragraph{C6: Peer-aware (L2).}
Each agent additionally receives a sentence establishing that it is
working with a \emph{partner} who holds complementary information,
and that the goal is to solve the problem together. This activates
awareness of the collaborative situation without specifying a
cognitive stance.

\paragraph{C7: Student-sim (L3).}
Each agent is instructed to adopt the communicative stance of an active
student: express uncertainty explicitly, ask clarifying questions
before proceeding to solution steps, acknowledge when a step is unclear,
and treat the partner's contributions as material to reason about rather
than accept at face value. Agents are told that producing an
authoritative-sounding response is not the goal---shared understanding
is. This framing targets observable discourse behaviour (question-asking,
uncertainty expression, joint model-building) rather than attributing a
belief state to the model.

\paragraph{Scale and replication.}
Each of the 140 problems was run under each of the three conditions,
with between 3 and 7 replications per problem-condition pair using
different random seeds (mean 4.7 replications), for a total of
$\mathbf{1{,}967}$ conversations. Per-problem mean \CDI{} values
(averaged over replications) are used as the unit of analysis for all
inferential tests.

\subsection{Design Scope}
\label{sec:scope}

The present study compares three conditions (C2, C6, C7) chosen to
vary epistemic framing from absent (C2) to peer-aware (C6) to
active-learner (C7). This three-condition design isolates the effect
of partner-awareness but does not disentangle active-learner
role framing from other strong role framings such as expert-solver or
debate framing, nor does it test whether framing effects persist
without the \CIDI{} information split. Two of these ablations---expert-solver
(C\textsubscript{EXP}) and full-context C7 without the jigsaw split
(C\textsubscript{FULL})---were run on the full 140-problem corpus;
results are reported in Section~\ref{sec:ablations}. A debate/critique
condition (C\textsubscript{DEB}) and an ignorance-only condition remain
as future work.

\subsection{Annotation Protocol}
\label{sec:annotation}

All conversations will be scored by two graduate-student annotators
(neither an author of this paper), trained on the \CPP{} rubric over
two calibration sessions using held-out practice conversations.
Annotators will be blinded to condition assignment; condition labels
will be replaced with uninformative codes and conversations will be
presented in randomised order.
Label masking does not preclude inference from conversation content;
annotators may implicitly detect framing differences from discourse style.
Disagreements exceeding one scale point will be resolved by consensus
with a third annotator.

\paragraph{Inter-rater reliability.}
Reliability is assessed on a stratified sample of 36 conversations
(3 conditions $\times$ 4 quadrants $\times$ 3 conversations each),
providing condition- and quadrant-balanced coverage.
Table~\ref{tab:reliability} reports four target metrics aligned
with the conceptual structure of \CPP{} \citep{hayes2007answering}.
The 36-conversation calibration sample is complete (two annotators,
blinded to condition); four-way quadrant $\kappa$ already exceeds its
target ($\kappa = 0.769 > 0.60$). Ordinal-cell Krippendorff $\alpha$,
ICC$(2,1)$, and \CDI{} threshold $\kappa$ require full 12-cell ordinal
scoring and will be reported in the camera-ready version; expanding the
sample to $\geq 100$ conversations will be required for per-cell estimates.

\paragraph{Split and phase validation.}
Annotators also rated split genuineness (genuine vs.\ non-genuine),
depth, and clarity, and the binary presence of each \CPP{} process
phase (A--D) across the 36 calibration conversations.
Genuine splits received consistently higher depth and clarity scores
than non-genuine ones; $67\%$ of COUPLING and PROD\_FAIL conversations
carried genuine splits versus $0\%$ of TRIVIAL cases, confirming that
split quality predicts collaborative outcome.
As preliminary evidence of automated scoring validity, human \CDI{}
showed a moderate correlation with LLM-assigned \CDI{}
($r = 0.45$, $R^2 = 0.20$); full validation at the ordinal-cell level
requires $\geq 100$ calibration conversations (see L4).
At the phase level, Phase~A was least frequently activated
(${\sim}39\%$ of conversations) and most reliably coded
($\kappa = 0.70$), confirming it as the most discriminative marker
of deep collaboration; Phase~C reached near-ceiling activation
($83$--$100\%$), limiting its discriminative value.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Hypothesis 1: Framing Is Associated with Greater Collaborative Depth}

Table~\ref{tab:main_results} reports mean scores for all metrics
across the three conditions. C7 yields dramatically higher \CDI{} than
both C2 ($\Delta = +0.251$) and C6 ($\Delta = +0.222$). The \CQI{}
increase is similarly large ($+0.106$ over C2), as is the improvement
in \ATC{} ($+0.096$). The \CY{} composite rises by $+0.134$ over the
baseline. All pairwise differences between C7 and C2, and between C7
and C6, are statistically significant with Wilcoxon signed-rank tests
on per-problem mean \CDI{} values ($n = 140$ pairs per comparison;
Table~\ref{tab:stats}).

Critically, the C6 vs.\ C2 comparison yields a Cohen's $d$ of only
$0.16$ ($p = 0.026$), which does \emph{not} survive Bonferroni
correction for three comparisons ($\alpha/3 = 0.0167$). The effect is
negligible in practical terms. Peer-awareness alone does not shift
agents away from expert-answering behavior---it only marginally changes
\CDI{} relative to the natural baseline. This rules out simple partner-awareness as a sufficient explanation,
consistent with the active-learner communicative stance being the primary
framing component associated with \CDI{} gains (see also Section~\ref{sec:ablations}).

\subsection{Hierarchical Model: Controlling for Difficulty and Subject}
\label{sec:lmm}

To address the nested structure (replications within problems within
subject-difficulty cells), we fit a linear mixed-effects model with
random intercepts per problem and fixed effects for condition,
difficulty level, and subject area. Condition effects are qualitatively
unchanged from the Wilcoxon tests: C7 ($\hat{\beta} = +0.251$,
$p < 0.001$), C6 ($\hat{\beta} = +0.028$, $p = 0.011$, not significant
after Bonferroni correction). The problem random intercept accounts for
$31\%$ of total \CDI{} variance---non-negligible, and controlling for
it strengthens rather than weakens the condition effect estimates.

\subsection{Hypothesis 2: Phase A Activation as Observable Correlate of Deep Collaboration}

The \PhAQ{} metric isolates the quality of the joint exploration
phase. Under C2, $67.9\%$ of conversations included any Phase~A
activity ($\PhAQ > 0$); under C6 this rises modestly to $74.3\%$.
Under C7, however, $99.3\%$ of conversations exhibit substantive Phase~A
engagement (139 of 140 problems; $d = 1.22$ for C7 vs.\ C2,
$p < 0.001$). Mean \PhAQ{} under C7 ($0.135$) is $2.5\times$ that
under C2 ($0.053$), indicating not just more frequent Phase~A activity
but higher quality joint exploration.

Phase~A activation is the strongest observable correlate of the C7
framing pattern: the active-learner stance makes presenting an
immediate solution communicatively inappropriate, promoting joint
negotiation of shared understanding first. Whether this reflects a
causal mechanism or a prompted discourse pattern requires micro-level
trace analysis.

\subsection{Quadrant Distribution}

Figure~\ref{fig:quadrants} and Table~\ref{tab:quadrants} show the
distribution of conversations across the four outcome quadrants. The
most striking shift is from COLLAPSE to PROD\_FAIL: under C2,
$46\%$ of conversations fall in the COLLAPSE quadrant (low process,
incorrect); under C7, this drops to $18\%$. The COUPLING quadrant
(high process, correct) grows from $11\%$ to $24\%$. The PROD\_FAIL
quadrant grows from $25\%$ to $49\%$, indicating that the student-role
framing substantially increases the rate of productive failure.

The large PROD\_FAIL mass under C7 reflects corpus difficulty, not
framing failure: Phase~1 selected problems that challenge even deep
collaboration. The evaluatively decisive comparison is against COLLAPSE
($46\% \to 18\%$, $2.5\times$ reduction under C7), which signals the
framing prevents unproductive engagement rather than merely
redistributing incorrect outcomes.
Both pairwise distribution shifts are statistically significant
($\chi^2(3)$ tests based on per-condition $n\approx 655$:
C7 vs.\ C2 $\chi^2 \approx 183$, C7 vs.\ C6 $\chi^2 \approx 120$,
both $p < 0.001$); the C6 vs.\ C2 shift does not reach significance
($\chi^2 \approx 8$, $p = 0.05$), consistent with the negligible
effect on \CDI{}.

\subsection{Epistemic Decomposition: Agent Epistemic Contribution}
\label{sec:aec}

The metrics presented so far measure \emph{how much} agents collaborate
(\CDI) and \emph{how deeply} they engage across phases (\CQI, \PhAQ).
They do not answer a more fundamental question: was collaboration
epistemically \emph{necessary}---that is, did the pair achieve a result
neither agent could reach alone? We address this via a Shapley-value
decomposition we term Agent Epistemic Contribution (\AEC).

\paragraph{Setup.}
Each agent runs in isolation with only its own \CIDI{} packet (GPT-4.1,
$T=0$ for deterministic baselines; collaborative sessions used $T=0.7$).
Three value functions on $\{0, 0.5, 1\}$: $v(\{A\})$, $v(\{B\})$, and
$v(\{A,B\}) = \text{best C7 replication correctness}$; standard $N=2$
Shapley decomposition yields $\AEC_A$, $\AEC_B$ (formula in
Appendix~\ref{app:robustness}). Three aggregate measures: \EN{}
(pair surpasses both solos); \CS{} (gain over best solo);
EB (Shapley balance). The decomposition separates \emph{structural}
from \emph{activated} interdependence---a distinction invisible to \CDI{} alone \citep{swiecki2021measuring}.

\paragraph{Results.}
Table~\ref{tab:aec} summarises the decomposition. Solo correctness is
very low: $v_A = 0.100$, $v_B = 0.057$---consistent with the \CIDI{}
design goal that neither packet alone is solvable. The C7 pair achieves
$v_{AB} = 0.357$, a $3.6\times$ improvement over the better individual.
Critically, $85\%$ of problems exhibit \emph{both-solo-zero}: neither
packet provides sufficient information for any correct answer, validating
the \CIDI{} epistemic filter at the correctness level. \EN{} holds in
$25\%$ of problems---cases where collaboration not only was structurally
required but also succeeded. Mean \CS{} $= +0.207$ and mean EB $=
0.857$: when collaboration succeeds, the gains are substantial and
both agents contribute comparably.

  EB: Shapley balance between agents.}

\paragraph{Connecting AEC to framing.}
The $60$-percentage-point gap between structural interdependence ($85\%$
both-solo-zero) and activated epistemic necessity ($25\%$ EN under C7)
is invisible to accuracy metrics, and quantifies headroom available to
better framing or coordination mechanisms. High both-solo-zero with low
\EN{} is a diagnostic signal that collaborative task structure is unactivated.

\subsection{Robustness and Validity}
\label{sec:robustness}

C7 numerically outperforms C2 in all 30 subject $\times$ difficulty cells
(Appendix~\ref{app:heatmap}); per-cell $n$ is too small for formal
cell-level testing, so this is \emph{descriptive robustness, not formal
generalization}.
Four validity checks (details in Appendix~\ref{app:robustness}):
partner-awareness alone is insufficient (C6 vs.\ C2: $d=0.16$,
$p=0.026$, not surviving Bonferroni correction);
\CDI{} is orthogonal to correctness ($\chi^2=0.01$, $p=0.94$),
and the C7 COUPLING advantage holds at equal mean turn count ($19.9$);
DAG-weighted \CDI$_w$ preserves condition rankings ($\rho>0.99$);
bootstrap $95\%$ CI for $\Delta_{\CDI}$(C7$-$C2) $= [0.219, 0.284]$,
$n=140$ power $>99.9\%$ at $d=1.30$.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Design implications.}
The C6 $\approx$ C2 finding ($d = 0.16$, not significant after Bonferroni correction)
shows that telling agents they have a collaborative partner \citep{liang2023encouraging,
du2023improving} leaves the default expert-answering mode largely intact in the
\CIDI-amenable regime. C\textsubscript{EXP} (expert framing with \CIDI{} split)
produces the highest COLLAPSE rate across all five conditions, consistent with
the active-learner stance being the framing component most strongly associated with \CDI{} gains.
For benchmark designers, the 60-point gap between structural
($85\%$ both-solo-zero) and activated ($25\%$ EN under C7) interdependence
captured by \AEC{} is invisible to accuracy metrics: high both-solo-zero with
low \EN{} signals that framing is failing to exploit the task's collaborative structure.
The ablations reinforce this: neither component alone suffices
(§\ref{sec:ablations})---C\textsubscript{FULL} promotes independent solving
($49\%$ TRIVIAL) while C\textsubscript{EXP} suppresses epistemic inquiry ($53\%$ COLLAPSE).

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Several limitations bound the current conclusions.
\emph{(L1) Corpus conditionality}: all estimates are conditional on
\CIDI-amenable problems (corpus filtered using C7; see §4.1); effect
sizes may overestimate the benefit expected on an unfiltered sample
(post-hoc validation on $67$ rejected problems shows symmetric collapse,
inconsistent with filter-induced inflation; §4.1).
\emph{(L2) Remaining scope}: subcomponent ablations (ignorance-only, debate framing) remain; \AEC{} EN and CS are upper-bound estimates under C7 (best replication), requiring per-condition runs for cross-condition comparison.
\emph{(L3) Model generalisability}: all agents use GPT-4.1; framing
effects may differ for other model families or fine-tuned models.
\emph{(L4) Reliability sample}: the $n=36$ stratified calibration sample
is complete (§4.4); quadrant $\kappa{=}0.769$ exceeds the $0.60$ target.
Ordinal-cell Krippendorff $\alpha$, ICC$(2,1)$, and \CDI{} threshold
$\kappa$ require full 12-cell ordinal scoring ($\geq 100$ conversations for
per-cell estimates) and will be reported in the camera-ready version.
The $r=0.45$ CDI correlation reported in §4.4 is preliminary evidence;
inferential tests in §5 use LLM-assigned \CDI{} and should be interpreted
accordingly.
\emph{(L5) Metric alignment}: \CPP{} rubric competencies (shared
understanding, joint action, team organisation) partially overlap by
design with the discourse behaviours C7 explicitly prompts (uncertainty
expression, clarification, uptake). Whether \CPP{} captures genuine
collaborative reasoning or rubric-compliant surface behaviour therefore
cannot be fully resolved without micro-level semantic analysis. The
C6 $\approx$ C2 finding ($d{=}0.16$) is consistent with genuine
activation beyond surface compliance, but does not close this confound;
trace analysis remains needed.

% ================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We introduced \CPP{} and \CIDI{} to measure how deeply LLM agents
collaborate, and showed that epistemic role framing (active-learner stance) is associated
with a substantial shift from expert-answering toward collaborative
sense-making ($d = 1.30$ on \CDI; COUPLING more than doubles). Partner-awareness
alone does not produce this shift ($d = 0.16$, not significant after
Bonferroni correction), and C7 numerically outperforms C2 in all 30
subject-difficulty cells (descriptive; per-cell samples are small).
\AEC{} reveals that \CIDI{} creates structural interdependence ($85\%$
both-solo-zero) while only $25\%$ becomes activated epistemic necessity
under C7---a gap invisible to accuracy-based evaluation.

For the Evaluation and Design (E\&D) community: \CDI{} and the structural/activated distinction add diagnostic value beyond accuracy (which conflates COUPLING with TRIVIAL and misses PROD\_FAIL). Active-learner framing is an effective design intervention in the \CIDI-amenable regime, and the ablation conditions support the interpretation that its effect is synergistic with the \CIDI{} split---neither component alone suffices.
An anonymized review release is available; see Appendix~\ref{app:artifacts} for full artifact documentation.

\section{CPP Annotation Rubric}
\label{app:rubric}

Each of the 12 cells $(P, C)$ for $P \in \{A, B, C, D\}$ and
$C \in \{1, 2, 3\}$ is scored on a 0--3 scale. We provide here the
anchoring descriptors for selected high-diagnostic cells.

\paragraph{Cell A1 (Exploring \& Understanding / Shared Understanding).}
\textbf{0}: No evidence that agents jointly discussed the problem;
one agent simply began solving. \textbf{1}: Agents acknowledged the
problem exists and restated it, but no reconciliation of perspectives.
\textbf{2}: Agents explicitly compared what each knows and flagged
gaps; at least one question about the other's packet was asked and
answered. \textbf{3}: Full joint problem model constructed; both
agents articulate a shared interpretation that differs from what
either could have produced alone, and this model visibly shapes the
subsequent solution strategy.

\paragraph{Cell D3 (Monitoring \& Reflecting / Team Organisation).}
\textbf{0}: No meta-discussion of the collaborative process or
strategy; agents proceed to output without review. \textbf{1}: One
agent flags a potential error but the flag is ignored by the partner.
\textbf{2}: Agents engage in a substantive exchange about whether
their approach is working; at least one revision of strategy is
proposed. \textbf{3}: Agents explicitly evaluate the quality of their
collaboration itself (e.g., ``I think we should have started from your
constraint, not mine---let's restart that step'') and implement a
correction.

\paragraph{Full rubric.} The complete 12-cell rubric with all anchor
descriptors, boundary case examples, and annotator guidelines is
available in the supplementary material.

\section{CIDI Generation Prompt}
\label{app:cidi_prompt}

The \CIDI{} split is generated using the following structured prompt
template with GPT-4.1 at temperature 0. The prompt instructs the
generator to:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Identify the core mathematical objects and relations in the
  problem.
  \item Partition those objects into two groups such that group~A
  provides the structural constraints and group~B provides the
  quantitative anchors (or a domain-appropriate analogue).
  \item Verify that a solver with only group~A information cannot
  determine the numerical answer even with unlimited mathematical
  knowledge, and vice versa.
  \item Express each packet in natural language, avoiding explicit
  statements of what is absent from the other packet.
\end{enumerate}
The generated split is validated by running a single GPT-4.1 agent
on each packet alone; if the agent reaches the correct answer, the
split is regenerated with tighter constraints. Typical regeneration
rate was $12\%$ of problems.

\section{Pilot Study: Condition Selection}
\label{app:pilot}

The scale study conditions (C2, C6, C7) were selected on the basis of
a pilot study involving 7 conditions (C1--C7) run on 4 anchor
problems across 3 replications ($= 84$ conversations). C1 (no
jigsaw, no framing) was eliminated because \CDI{} was uniformly near
zero. C3 (constitutional split, negative instruction) and C5 (C3 +
peer-aware) were eliminated because agents frequently bypassed the
information restriction, invalidating the independence manipulation.
C4 (CIDI split + joint accountability framing) was eliminated due to a
design conflict: the joint accountability instruction asked agents to
share responsibility for a combined answer, which undermined the
reasoning-output dependency that the CIDI split requires. The pilot
established an ordering C7 $>$ C4 $>$ C2 $>$ C6 $>$ C3 $>$ C5 $>$
C1 for \CDI{}, motivating the three-way comparison in the scale study.
The unexpected result that C6 fell below C2 in the pilot (in the full
study, C6 scores marginally above C2, $d=0.16$, but does not survive
Bonferroni correction) first alerted us to the practically null effect
of peer-awareness.

\section{Robustness and Validity Checks — Details}
\label{app:robustness}

\paragraph{AEC Shapley formula.}
For a two-agent system, the standard Shapley value decomposition with
value function $v : 2^{\{A,B\}} \to [0,1]$ (where $v(\emptyset)=0$,
$v(\{A\})$ and $v(\{B\})$ are solo correctness rates, and
$v(\{A,B\})$ is the pair correctness rate) yields:
\begin{align}
  \AEC_A &= \tfrac{1}{2}\,v(\{A\}) + \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl[v(\{A,B\}) - v(\{B\})\bigr], \\
  \AEC_B &= \tfrac{1}{2}\,v(\{B\}) + \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl[v(\{A,B\}) - v(\{A\})\bigr].
\end{align}
By construction $\AEC_A + \AEC_B = v(\{A,B\})$ (efficiency).
Epistemic Necessity (\EN) holds for a problem when $v(\{A,B\}) > \max(v(\{A\}), v(\{B\}))$.
Collaborative Surplus (\CS) $= v(\{A,B\}) - \max(v(\{A\}), v(\{B\}))$.
Epistemic Balance (EB) $= 1 - |{\AEC_A - \AEC_B}| / v(\{A,B\})$ (defined when $v(\{A,B\}) > 0$).
In the reported corpus, correctness is binary per replication; we use
the best-replication C7 value as $v(\{A,B\})$ (upper-bound estimate;
per-condition \AEC{} requires separate condition-specific solo runs).

\paragraph{W2 — C6 rules out partner-awareness, not ignorance.}
C6 establishes explicit partner-awareness without any learner-role
component. If simulated ignorance alone explained C7's gains, C6
should outperform C2 (since C6 agents equally lack full-solution access).
The negligible C6 vs.\ C2 effect ($d=0.16$, $\Delta_{\CDI}=+0.028$,
$p=0.026$, not surviving Bonferroni correction) rules out simple partner-awareness
as a sufficient mechanism. C6 does \emph{not} fully isolate the active-learner
stance from expert-certainty withholding; an ignorance-only ablation
(``reason cautiously; you may not have the full answer'' without
collaboration directives) remains a key future experiment.

\paragraph{W3 — CDI and correctness are orthogonal (by design).}
Across 1,967 conversations, $P(\text{correct} \mid \CDI \geq 0.5)
= 0.321$ vs.\ $P(\text{correct} \mid \CDI < 0.5) = 0.324$
($\chi^2 = 0.01$, $p = 0.94$). \CDI{} measures process quality, not
outcome quality. The C7 COUPLING advantage ($24\%$ vs.\ $11\%$ C2)
holds at identical mean conversation length (19.9 turns across all
conditions): C7 achieves $0.031$ \CDI{} per turn vs.\ $0.018$ for C2,
ruling out a length confound.

\paragraph{W4 — Convergent validity with DAG-weighted CDI.}
A topology-weighted variant $\CDI_w$ (phase depth $\times$ competency
depth, normalized) yields $\CDI_w = 0.369, 0.397, 0.601$ for C2, C6,
C7 (vs.\ uniform $0.362, 0.390, 0.613$). Spearman $\rho > 0.99$ across
all 1,967 conversations confirms that condition rankings are robust to
the specific weighting scheme.

\paragraph{W5 — Power and confidence intervals.}
Bootstrap $95\%$ CI for $\Delta_\CDI$(C7$-$C2) $= [0.219, 0.284]$,
entirely above zero. $n=140$ problems provides $>99.9\%$ power at
$d=1.30$, $\alpha=0.05$. Within-problem replication variance
($\sigma_{\text{within}} \approx 0.23$) implies single-problem \CDI{}
estimates carry substantial uncertainty ($95\%$ CI $\approx \pm 0.26$).

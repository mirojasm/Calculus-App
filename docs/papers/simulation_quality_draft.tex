\subsection{1. Introduction}\label{introduction}

\subsubsection{1.1 The Collaboration Quality
Gap}\label{the-collaboration-quality-gap}

The CollabMath framework demonstrated that L3 student-simulation agents
(C7) significantly outperform natural agents (C2) in epistemic
collaboration quality (CDI: 0.613 vs 0.362, d=1.30). Yet even in the
best condition, only 24\% of agent pairs reach genuine collaborative
success (COUPLING quadrant). The majority fail:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{49\% PROD\_FAIL}: agents engage epistemically but do not
  converge on a correct answer
\item
  \textbf{18\% TRIVIAL}: agents solve in parallel without genuine
  collaboration
\item
  \textbf{9\% COLLAPSE}: agents fail to engage meaningfully
\end{itemize}

This paper asks: what prevents LLM agents from collaborating better,
even with ideal information structure and epistemic framing?

\subsubsection{1.2 Research Questions}\label{research-questions}

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{RQ1 --- Failure mode analysis}: What are the proximate causes
  of COLLAPSE, PROD\_FAIL, and TRIVIAL in LLM agent pairs?
\item
  \textbf{RQ2 --- Phase A activation}: Why does PhAQ remain low
  (mean=0.135) even when Phase A is technically activated (99.3\% of
  conversations)?
\item
  \textbf{RQ3 --- Intervention efficacy}: Which prompting or protocol
  interventions improve CDI and COUPLING rate without invalidating the
  existing CPP framework?
\item
  \textbf{RQ4 --- Model scale}: Does using a more capable base LLM
  (e.g., GPT-4.1 vs GPT-4o-mini for simulation) substantially change
  collaboration quality?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{2. Background}\label{background}

\subsubsection{2.1 Collaborative Learning
Theory}\label{collaborative-learning-theory}

\textbf{Positive interdependence} (Johnson \& Johnson, 1989):
collaboration is only genuine when group members cannot succeed
independently. The CollabMath framework enforces this via information
splits (jigsaw).

\textbf{Grounding and Phase A} (Roschelle \& Teasley, 1995; Fischer et
al., 2013): effective collaboration requires an initial phase of shared
understanding construction before problem-solving. This is Phase A in
the CPP framework --- agents must establish a joint problem
representation before executing.

\textbf{Transactive discourse} (Berkowitz \& Gibbs, 1983): high-quality
collaboration involves agents building directly on each other's
reasoning, not just turn-taking. The CQI metric captures this.

\textbf{Productive failure} (Kapur, 2016): incorrect collaborative
attempts that activate deep epistemic engagement may produce better
learning outcomes than correct solo attempts. The PROD\_FAIL quadrant
may be more valuable than its label suggests.

\subsubsection{2.2 LLM Multi-Agent
Collaboration}\label{llm-multi-agent-collaboration}

{[}CITE: relevant work on LLM multi-agent systems --- MetaGPT Hong 2023,
Camel Li 2023, AutoGen Wu 2023, Society of Mind Park 2023{]}

Key gap: existing multi-agent frameworks optimize for task completion,
not epistemic collaboration quality. None of them measure or target
Phase A behavior.

\subsubsection{2.3 Why LLM Agents Fail at Epistemic
Collaboration}\label{why-llm-agents-fail-at-epistemic-collaboration}

Hypotheses (to be tested):

\textbf{H-A (Competence escape)}: When an agent can partially solve the
problem from its own information, it bypasses Phase A. The jigsaw split
forces information-level dependence but not reasoning-level dependence.

\textbf{H-B (Authority acceptance)}: LLM agents trained on human text
are biased toward accepting partner statements rather than challenging
them (sycophancy from RLHF). This suppresses Phase A.

\textbf{H-C (Protocol gap)}: Without explicit turn structure, agents
default to efficient information exchange rather than epistemic
exploration. The C7 prompt allows but does not force Phase A.

\textbf{H-D (Scale effects)}: GPT-4o-mini (used for simulation) may lack
the metacognitive capacity to sustain Phase A behavior under cognitive
load.

\subsection{4. Proposed Interventions}\label{proposed-interventions}

\subsubsection{4.1 Structured Turn Protocol
(STP)}\label{structured-turn-protocol-stp}

\textbf{Hypothesis}: Explicitly requiring Phase A before Phase B
prevents premature solution attempts.

\textbf{Implementation}: Add a mandatory turn structure to the C7 system
prompt:

\begin{verbatim}
Turn 1: Share your information packet only. Do NOT attempt to solve.
Turn 2: Ask your partner at least one verification question about their information.
Turn 3: Answer your partner's question. Challenge any inconsistency.
Turn 4+: Collaborate on the solution.
\end{verbatim}

\textbf{Expected effect}: Higher PhAQ (forced Phase A), potentially
lower CDI if it introduces rigidity.

\textbf{Risk}: May reduce naturalistic collaboration, inflate PhAQ
without improving epistemic quality.

\subsubsection{4.2 Epistemic Skepticism Prompting
(ESP)}\label{epistemic-skepticism-prompting-esp}

\textbf{Hypothesis}: LLM sycophancy suppresses Phase A. An explicit
anti-sycophancy instruction (``assume your partner may have made an
error'') activates challenge behavior.

\textbf{Implementation}: Add to C7 system prompt:

\begin{verbatim}
IMPORTANT: Do not assume your partner's information or calculations are correct.
Before using any value they provide, verify it against your own packet.
If something seems inconsistent, say so explicitly.
\end{verbatim}

\textbf{Expected effect}: Higher Phase A quality, more genuine
challenges, potentially more PROD\_FAIL (agents challenge even correct
info).

\subsubsection{4.3 Socratic Prompting}\label{socratic-prompting}

\textbf{Hypothesis}: Replacing direct information exchange with
question-based interaction forces deeper engagement.

\textbf{Implementation}: Agents must ask questions to elicit partner's
information rather than receive it directly. Partner answers questions
instead of sharing packets.

\subsubsection{4.4 Model Scale Ablation}\label{model-scale-ablation}

\textbf{Hypothesis}: GPT-4o-mini lacks metacognitive capacity for
sustained Phase A; GPT-4.1 would produce substantially better
collaboration.

\textbf{Design}: Replicate C7 condition with GPT-4.1 as simulation model
on n=30 problems (cost: \textasciitilde\$15).

\textbf{Expected effect}: Higher PhAQ, more COUPLING. If model scale is
the main bottleneck, this is evidence for using stronger models in
future work.

\subsection{6. Expected Results and
Significance}\label{expected-results-and-significance}

\subsubsection{6.1 Best-case scenario}\label{best-case-scenario}

One or more interventions significantly improves CDI (d\textgreater0.5)
and COUPLING rate (\textgreater35\%). This establishes that
collaboration quality is malleable through prompting --- it is not
purely a property of the split quality or model capability.

\subsubsection{6.2 Null result scenario}\label{null-result-scenario}

No intervention significantly improves CDI. This would indicate that
model scale (H-D) or the fundamental structure of the task is the
bottleneck, not prompting. This is also a publishable result --- it
bounds what prompting can achieve.

\subsubsection{6.3 Significance for the
field}\label{significance-for-the-field}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  First systematic analysis of LLM collaboration failure modes at the
  PISA CPS rubric level
\item
  Establishes what aspects of epistemic collaboration can be improved
  through prompting vs.~require architectural changes
\item
  Provides a protocol for evaluating future multi-agent systems on
  collaboration quality (not just task accuracy)
\end{itemize}
